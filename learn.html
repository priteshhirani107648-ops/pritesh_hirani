ASIC Design Flow

Physical Design (PD) is the process of transforming a logical circuit description (RTL/Netlist) into a physical layout that can be manufactured. It bridges the gap between front-end logic design and back-end fabrication.

Core Objectives

  • PPA Optimization: Balancing Power, Performance (Speed/Timing), and Area.
  • Manufacturability: Ensuring the final GDSII is free of Design Rule Check (DRC) violations.
  • Signal Integrity: Preventing crosstalk, EM (Electromigration), and excessive IR drop.

Floorplanning & Power

Floorplanning is the most critical stage. A bad floorplan will cause unfixable routing congestion and timing failures later in the flow.

Key Operations

  • Core & Die Sizing: Establishing the physical boundaries and determining the aspect ratio and core utilization (typically starting around 60-70%).
  • Macro Placement: Positioning large IP blocks (SRAMs, PLLs). Macros are typically placed near the boundaries to keep the center open for standard cells, reducing wire routing detours.
  • Power Planning (PG Grid): Building a robust mesh of metal layers (VDD and VSS) to deliver clean power to every cell. We use upper thick metal layers to minimize resistance and combat IR Drop.

Standard Cell Placement

Placement involves assigning exact coordinates to millions of standard cells within the core area defined during floorplanning. This is done in two main phases:

1. Global Placement

The tool determines approximate locations for all cells to minimize total wirelength and meet timing constraints. Cells may overlap during this stage.

2. Detail Placement

The tool legalizes the placement. It snaps cells to the placement grid (site rows) and ensures absolutely no cells are overlapping. It also resolves local routing congestion.

High Fanout Net Synthesis (HFNS)

During placement, nets with thousands of driven cells (like reset lines) are buffered to prevent massive transition violations and delays.

Clock Tree Synthesis

Before CTS, the clock is considered "ideal" (zero delay). CTS builds a physical buffer tree to distribute the clock signal from the root pin to every sequential element (flop) in the design simultaneously.

Primary CTS Goals

  • Minimize Skew: Skew is the difference in clock arrival time between two communicating flops. High skew directly causes Setup and Hold timing violations.
  • Minimize Insertion Delay: The total time it takes for the clock to travel from the root to the leaf nodes. High latency increases the chip's susceptibility to OCV (On-Chip Variation).
  • Power Dissipation: The clock network toggles constantly and consumes up to 40% of the chip's total dynamic power. Clock Gating is utilized to shut down inactive branches.

Routing Phase

Routing connects the placed cells using the metal layers available in the foundry's technology node, following the logical connections defined in the netlist.

The Routing Flow

  • Global Routing: The core is divided into grid cells (GCells). The tool plans approximate paths for nets to avoid congested areas.
  • Track Assignment: Nets are assigned to specific routing tracks on specific metal layers.
  • Detail Routing: The actual physical metal geometries and vias are drawn. The router strictly adheres to foundry Design Rules (spacing, min width) to prevent shorts and spacing violations.

Signoff & Verification

Before the layout can be sent to the foundry (Tapeout), it must pass rigorous mathematical and physical checks to guarantee it will function as manufactured.

1. Static Timing Analysis (STA)

Using tools like PrimeTime to verify that the design meets the target frequency across all Multi-Mode Multi-Corner (MMMC) scenarios without Setup, Hold, or Transition violations.

2. Physical Verification (Calibre)

  • DRC (Design Rule Check): Confirms no metal tracks are too close together or too thin.
  • LVS (Layout vs. Schematic): Extracts the physical shapes back into a netlist and compares it against the original logical netlist to ensure no missing or shorted connections.
