ASIC Design Flow

Physical Design (PD) is the process of transforming a logical circuit description (RTL/Netlist) into a physical layout that can be manufactured. It bridges the gap between front-end logic design and back-end fabrication.

Core Objectives

  • PPA Optimization: Balancing Power, Performance (Speed/Timing), and Area constraints across the entire chip.
  • Manufacturability: Ensuring the final GDSII stream is entirely free of Design Rule Check (DRC) violations before tapeout.
  • Signal Integrity: Preventing crosstalk noise, EM (Electromigration), and excessive dynamic/static IR drop.

Floorplanning & Power

Floorplanning is the most critical stage of the PD lifecycle. A flawed floorplan will invariably cause unfixable routing congestion and timing closures failures later in the flow.

Key Operations

  • Core & Die Sizing: Establishing the physical boundaries and determining the aspect ratio and core utilization (typically initiating around 60-70% to leave room for buffers and routing).
  • Macro Placement: Positioning large IP blocks (SRAMs, PLLs, ADCs). Macros are typically placed near the boundaries to keep the center open for standard cells, dramatically reducing wire routing detours.
  • Power Planning (PG Grid): Building a robust, multi-layered mesh of metal tracks (VDD and VSS) to deliver clean power to every standard cell. We utilize upper thick metal layers (e.g., M7/M8) to minimize resistance and combat IR Drop.

Standard Cell Placement

Placement involves assigning exact geometric coordinates to millions of standard cells within the core area defined during floorplanning. This is computationally intense and done in phases:

1. Global Placement

The EDA tool calculates approximate locations for all cells to minimize total wirelength and meet timing constraints. Cells are allowed to overlap during this macro-level stage.

2. Detail Placement

The tool "legalizes" the placement. It snaps cells perfectly into the placement grid (site rows) and ensures absolutely no standard cells are overlapping. It also resolves localized routing congestion hotspots.

High Fanout Net Synthesis (HFNS)

During the placement phase, nets with thousands of driven cells (like reset lines or scan enables) are heavily buffered to prevent massive transition violations, signal degradation, and logic delays.

Clock Tree Synthesis

Before CTS, the clock is considered "ideal" (zero delay, infinite drive). CTS builds a physical buffer tree to distribute the clock signal from the root pin to every sequential element (flop) in the design simultaneously.

Primary CTS Goals

  • Minimize Skew: Skew is the difference in clock arrival time between two communicating flops. High skew directly causes severe Setup and Hold timing violations.
  • Minimize Insertion Delay: The total time it takes for the clock to travel from the root port to the leaf nodes. High latency increases the chip's susceptibility to OCV (On-Chip Variation).
  • Power Dissipation: The clock network toggles constantly and consumes up to 40% of the chip's total dynamic power. Clock Gating cells (ICGs) are utilized to shut down inactive branches to save power.

Routing Phase

Routing connects the placed standard cells and macros using the metal layers available in the foundry's technology node, strictly adhering to the logical connections defined in the netlist.

The Routing Flow

  • Global Routing: The core is divided into a grid of GCells. The tool plans approximate mathematical paths for nets to navigate around highly congested areas.
  • Track Assignment: Nets are officially assigned to specific routing tracks on specific metal layers (e.g., vertical on M4, horizontal on M5).
  • Detail Routing: The actual physical metal geometries and vias are drawn. The router strictly adheres to foundry Design Rules (spacing, minimum width, via enclosures) to prevent shorts, opens, and spacing violations.

Signoff & Verification

Before the layout can be sent to the foundry for fabrication (Tapeout), it must pass rigorous mathematical, electrical, and physical checks to guarantee it will function as manufactured.

1. Static Timing Analysis (STA)

Using tools like Synopsys PrimeTime to verify that the design meets the target frequency across all Multi-Mode Multi-Corner (MMMC) scenarios (SS, FF, TT) without Setup, Hold, or Transition violations.

2. Physical Verification (Calibre)

  • DRC (Design Rule Check): Confirms no metal tracks are too close together, too thin, or improperly aligned according to the foundry's exact manufacturing limits.
  • LVS (Layout vs. Schematic): Extracts the physical shapes back into a simulated netlist and compares it against the original logical netlist to ensure there are no missing or shorted connections.
  • Antenna Checks: Ensures long metal traces do not accumulate static charge during plasma etching, which could blow out connected transistor gates.
